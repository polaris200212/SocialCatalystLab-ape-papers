# Section-by-Section Review

## The Opening
**Verdict:** [Solid but slightly academic / Needs a punchier hook]
The opening is professional and clear, but it misses the Shleifer "vivid observation" standard. It starts with the date of a legislative act.
*   **Feedback:** Shleifer would start with the *physical* or *human* reality of the 1930s South—the "pellagra and played-out soil"—rather than the signing of a bill. The second sentence is actually much stronger than the first.
*   **Suggested Rewrite:** "In 1933, the Tennessee Valley was a region of exhausted soil, endemic malaria, and deep poverty. Fewer than three percent of its farms had electricity. The Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA) was designed to change that—to turn a river into a powerhouse and, as Roosevelt promised, to touch 'every form of human concern' in the Valley."

## Introduction
**Verdict:** [Shleifer-ready / Exceptionally clear structure]
This is high-level economic writing. You frame the paper as a direct response to a canonical study (Kline and Moretti) and immediately explain the gap: "But Kline and Moretti studied counties, not people." This is a perfect transition.
*   **Detailed Feedback:** The transition from the "big push" theory to the Jim Crow reality is handled with great narrative energy (Glaeser-style). The preview of findings on page 3 is specific and quantitative.
*   **Improvement:** Page 4's contribution to "disaggregated census microdata" feels a bit like a "data advertisement." Shleifer would fold the data's power into the results themselves rather than listing it as a standalone third contribution.

- **Greatest weakness:** "Academic throat-clearing" in the early background sections (2.1 and 2.2).
- **Shleifer test:** Yes. A smart non-economist would be hooked by the end of page 2.

- **Top 5 concrete improvements:**
  1.  **Punch up the first sentence.** Move the "pellagra and played-out soil" to the very first line.
  2.  **Combine the "Contributions" section.** Instead of saying "Third, we demonstrate the power of microdata," say "Our use of microdata allows us to show, for the first time, that..." (Focus on the finding, not the tool).
  3.  **Visual Language in Results.** In Section 5.1, instead of "The mean Duncan Socioeconomic Index declined," try: "Occupational status for the average resident stalled—a paradox, given the rise of new factories."

  5.  **Active Voice Check.** Page 10: "Standard errors are clustered..." → "We cluster standard errors." Page 12: "Context is necessary." → "One must place these numbers in context." Always choose the actor over the abstraction.
