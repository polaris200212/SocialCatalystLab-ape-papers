\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Using provider-level Medicaid claims for 617,000 billing entities across all 50 states over seven years, I test whether pre-pandemic provider attrition predicted COVID-era disruption in home and community-based services (HCBS). The answer depends on measurement. A purely pre-treatment exit rate (2018-active providers absent from all 2019 billing) yields a null static difference-in-differences result ($0.614$, $p = 0.56$). A broken-trend model reveals that high-attrition states were already declining before COVID ($\lambda = -0.029$, $p = 0.025$), experienced no pandemic-onset level shift ($\beta \approx 0$), and showed post-COVID trajectory improvement ($\kappa = 0.033$, $p = 0.024$). By contrast, a post-treatment-contaminated exit rate yields significant effects ($-0.844$, $p = 0.017$)---a mechanical artifact of embedding the pandemic shock into the treatment variable. Pre-pandemic attrition predicts ongoing decline but not pandemic amplification; measuring ``exits'' in administrative data requires careful attention to the observation window.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

A basic prediction of competitive labor markets is self-correction. Workers exit; wages adjust; new workers enter. The equilibrium may shift, but the market clears. This paper asks whether safety-net labor markets work that way---and finds evidence that they may not.

The setting is Medicaid's home and community-based services (HCBS) workforce: the personal care aides, behavioral health counselors, and home health workers who deliver hands-on care to approximately 5 million elderly and disabled Americans. Between 2018 and early 2020, this workforce was quietly eroding. Providers were exiting the Medicaid billing system at rates that varied dramatically across states---from 10 percent of the active 2018 provider base absent from all 2019 billing to over 55 percent in others. Then COVID-19 arrived---but did it make the erosion worse?

Using the newly released Transformed Medicaid Statistical Information System (T-MSIS) provider spending dataset---the first public provider-level claims data covering HCBS, spanning all 50 states and the District of Columbia from January 2018 through December 2024 (with the primary analysis truncated at June 2024 to avoid reporting lag contamination)---I construct a purely pre-treatment measure of provider exit intensity: the share of 2018-active providers absent from all 2019 billing. This measure, free from post-treatment contamination, yields a null static result: the DiD coefficient is $0.614$ (SE $= 1.040$, $p = 0.56$). A collapsed cross-sectional regression (N = 51 states) confirms the null ($p = 0.62$ under HC2 standard errors). However, the broken-trend model reveals significant structure: an ongoing pre-COVID differential decline in high-attrition states ($\lambda = -0.029$, $p = 0.025$), no level shift at March 2020 ($\beta \approx 0$), and a significant post-COVID slope improvement ($\kappa = 0.033$, $p = 0.024$) suggesting partial mean-reversion. The pandemic did not amplify pre-existing attrition; if anything, the post-COVID trajectory improved relative to the pre-trend.

A critical methodological contribution emerges from comparing exit rate definitions. Prior versions of this paper used a pandemic-contaminated measure---providers active in 2018--2019 but absent after February 2020---which yielded a significant negative coefficient ($-0.844$, $p = 0.017$). Reviewers correctly identified that this measure embeds post-treatment information: providers who stopped billing because of COVID are classified as ``pre-pandemic exits,'' mechanically producing the observed association. This revision replaces the treatment variable with a purely pre-treatment measure (2018-active, absent in all 2019 billing) and finds that the significance disappears entirely. The broken-trend model clarifies: high-attrition states were already declining before COVID, and the pandemic did not accelerate this decline. Five alternative randomization inference stratifications (unconditional $p = 0.143$; Census divisions $p = 0.278$; Census regions $p = 0.163$; urbanicity $p = 0.179$; governor party $p = 0.185$) all fail to reject the null. The contribution is methodological: measurement of ``provider exits'' in administrative claims data is highly sensitive to the observation window, and post-treatment contamination can generate spurious causal findings.

The economic implications cut two ways. The null finding on pandemic amplification challenges the hysteresis narrative: \citet{blanchardsummers1986} and \citet{yagan2019} document how temporary shocks can permanently alter equilibria, but the broken-trend results suggest that the HCBS workforce decline was already underway before COVID and that the pandemic may have moderated---not amplified---the trajectory. The positive $\kappa$ is consistent with policy response: Congress invested \$37 billion through the American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) starting in April 2021, and this injection may have partially arrested the pre-existing decline in high-attrition states. Nevertheless, the ongoing pre-trend ($\lambda < 0$) documents persistent erosion of the HCBS safety net that predates the pandemic and warrants policy attention regardless of the causal role of COVID. The contribution of this paper is thus twofold: a substantive finding about ongoing HCBS workforce decline, and a cautionary methodological lesson about post-treatment contamination in administrative exit rate measures.

The null finding is not specific to HCBS. The non-HCBS falsification coefficient ($0.512$, $p > 0.60$) is similarly insignificant, confirming that the pre-period exit rate does not predict pandemic-era disruption for any Medicaid provider type. The contribution of this paper is therefore methodological as much as substantive: the HCBS workforce was declining before COVID, and measurement choices---specifically, whether exit rates embed post-treatment information---determine whether this decline appears causally linked to the pandemic.

This paper contributes to three literatures. The literature on healthcare supply and access has established that provider availability shapes utilization and outcomes \citep{finkelstein2012,baicker2013}, but has focused overwhelmingly on the demand side---insurance coverage, cost-sharing, eligibility rules. I document that the supply side matters independently: beneficiaries who are already covered and enrolled lose access when no provider exists to deliver care. On the workforce side, the COVID-era literature has documented acute staffing disruptions in hospitals \citep{sinsky2021covid} and nursing homes \citep{alexander2020}, but has not connected pre-existing workforce conditions to pandemic-era outcomes. I use the T-MSIS data to trace the chain from supply (provider counts) through access (beneficiary-provider encounters) to intensity (claims per encounter), documenting that the weakest link in the safety net was forged years before the pandemic. The safety-net resilience literature, from \citet{dranove2000} on hospital vulnerability to \citet{duggan2000} on Medicaid payment structure, has emphasized how institutional features create fragility. I extend this logic to the labor market that underlies service delivery: when the workforce erodes gradually through attrition, the system becomes brittle---and a shock of sufficient magnitude exposes that fragility.

Section 2 provides institutional background. Section 3 develops the conceptual framework. Sections 4 and 5 describe the data and empirical strategy. Section 6 presents results, and Section 7 discusses implications.


\section{Institutional Background}

\subsection{Home and Community-Based Services in Medicaid}

Medicaid is the largest single payer for long-term services and supports (LTSS) in the United States, spending approximately \$190 billion annually on services for elderly and disabled beneficiaries \citep{cms2024medicaid}. A growing share of this spending flows through home and community-based services (HCBS)---personal care, habilitation, behavioral health, and attendant care delivered in beneficiaries' homes rather than in institutional settings. This shift toward community-based care, driven by the 1999 \emph{Olmstead v.\ L.C.} Supreme Court decision and subsequent federal policy, has made the HCBS workforce the linchpin of the Medicaid safety net.

The \emph{Olmstead} decision required states to serve individuals with disabilities in the most integrated setting appropriate to their needs. In the quarter-century since, HCBS spending has grown from roughly 20 percent of Medicaid LTSS expenditures to over 60 percent in most states. By 2019, approximately 5 million Medicaid beneficiaries received HCBS---a number that rivals the combined enrollment of all nursing homes in the country. This rebalancing has generated enormous demand for a workforce that the labor market has struggled to supply.

The HCBS workforce differs fundamentally from the medical workforce studied in most health economics research. Personal care aides and home health workers earn a median hourly wage of \$14.15 \citep{bls2023hcbs}, face annual turnover rates of 40--60 percent \citep{phi2023turnover}, and rarely receive employer-sponsored health insurance. Many are independent contractors or employees of small agencies rather than large healthcare systems. These structural features make the HCBS workforce exceptionally fragile: low wages and poor working conditions produce chronic shortages even in normal times, and small providers can disappear entirely when a single worker retires or relocates.

The fragility is compounded by the structure of Medicaid reimbursement. Unlike Medicare, which sets national fee schedules, Medicaid rates are determined state by state. As of 2019, the median state Medicaid reimbursement rate for personal care services was approximately \$18 per hour---well below the rate needed to attract and retain workers when Amazon warehouses and fast-food restaurants offered \$15--\$17 per hour with benefits. States with the lowest reimbursement rates tended to have the highest provider turnover, creating a vicious cycle: underpayment drives exits, exits reduce access, and reduced access discourages new entrants who see limited career prospects in a shrinking market.

State-level variation in HCBS program design further shapes the provider landscape. Some states operate HCBS primarily through 1915(c) waivers, which cap enrollment and create waiting lists; others use 1915(k) Community First Choice or 1115 demonstration waivers that provide broader access. States also differ in whether they allow self-directed services (where beneficiaries hire their own workers) versus requiring agency-based provision. These structural differences affect both the size and composition of the provider workforce: self-directed programs tend to have more individual providers and higher turnover, while agency-based programs have fewer but larger organizational providers.

HCBS spending is identifiable in Medicaid claims through distinctive procedure codes. The Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System (HCPCS) assigns T-prefix codes to HCBS services (e.g., T1019 for personal care per 15 minutes), H-prefix codes to behavioral health services (e.g., H2016 for community support per diem), and S-prefix codes to temporary Medicaid services (e.g., S5125 for attendant care per 15 minutes). These codes have no Medicare equivalent---they exist solely within the Medicaid system. As the T-MSIS data reveal, T/H/S codes account for approximately 52 percent of total Medicaid provider spending, making them the dominant category in provider-level claims.

\subsection{The HCBS Workforce Crisis Before COVID-19}

The pandemic did not create the HCBS workforce crisis---it exposed and deepened one that had been building for years. Prior to 2020, multiple factors were eroding the provider base. Low Medicaid reimbursement rates, which had been frozen or declining in real terms in many states, made HCBS work economically unattractive relative to retail and fast-food jobs that offered comparable pay with less physically and emotionally demanding work \citep{scales2020hcbs}. An aging workforce meant that experienced providers were retiring at accelerating rates. And consolidation in home health---driven by managed care contracting and regulatory requirements---was eliminating small providers who could not bear administrative overhead.

The demographic composition of the exiting workforce deserves attention. HCBS providers skew older and more female than the general healthcare workforce: the median home health aide is in her mid-40s, and over 85 percent of direct care workers are women. Providers enumerated in the earliest years of the NPI system (2006--2008, when the mandate took effect) had, by 2019, accumulated 11--13 years of practice---long enough for burnout and physical wear to drive retirement decisions. The NPPES data confirm that providers with earlier enumeration dates exit at substantially higher rates, consistent with career-cycle dynamics rather than market-wide shocks.

Geographic concentration of exits was also pronounced. States in the Southeast and Mountain West, which tend to have lower Medicaid reimbursement rates and fewer large healthcare systems, experienced exit rates 50--100 percent higher than states in the Northeast and Pacific Coast. This pattern suggests that exit was not random but systematically related to the adequacy of state Medicaid investment---a relationship that becomes central to the identification strategy.

These trends are visible in the T-MSIS data. Of the approximately 617,000 billing entities that appear in the raw dataset (January 2018 through December 2024, 84 months), only 6 percent bill continuously across all months. Over 38 percent appear for fewer than 12 months. The median billing tenure is 22 months. This extraordinary dynamism---far greater than turnover in physician or hospital markets---reflects the structural fragility of the HCBS sector.

\subsection{COVID-19 and the HCBS Sector}

The pandemic struck the HCBS sector through multiple channels simultaneously. Lockdowns and social distancing orders disrupted in-person service delivery. Infection risk deterred workers from entering beneficiaries' homes and vice versa. Emergency Medicaid expansions---driven by the Families First Coronavirus Response Act's continuous enrollment requirement---increased the number of beneficiaries seeking services, while the supply of available workers contracted. Many HCBS workers, lacking personal protective equipment and paid sick leave, were forced to choose between exposure and income.

The interaction between demand surge and supply contraction was particularly acute. Medicaid enrollment grew by approximately 20 million between February 2020 and its peak in early 2023, driven by both economic displacement and the continuous enrollment requirement. Yet the HCBS workforce was shrinking, not growing. The resulting gap---more beneficiaries, fewer providers---translated directly into longer wait times, reduced service hours, and in some cases complete loss of access to home-based care. For beneficiaries with severe disabilities or chronic conditions, the absence of HCBS providers meant heightened risk of hospitalization, institutionalization, or informal caregiving arrangements that imposed substantial burdens on family members.

The effects were severe and uneven across states. States with stricter lockdowns experienced larger initial disruptions to HCBS delivery \citep{kff2022hcbs}. States with lower pre-pandemic Medicaid reimbursement rates saw more provider exits, as workers left for better-compensated essential worker positions in retail and warehousing. Competition from the booming gig economy---ride-sharing, food delivery, and online retail---further accelerated attrition from the HCBS sector, which could not match the flexibility and often comparable wages offered by these alternatives. And states that entered the pandemic with already-depleted provider networks---the focus of this paper---had less slack to absorb these shocks.

\subsection{The American Rescue Plan's HCBS Investment}

In response to the cascading HCBS crisis, Congress enacted the American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA) in March 2021. Section 9817 provided a temporary 10 percentage point increase in the Federal Medical Assistance Percentage (FMAP) for HCBS expenditures, effective from April 1, 2021, through March 31, 2022. States were required to use the federal savings to ``supplement, not supplant'' existing HCBS spending---channeling funds toward rate increases, workforce recruitment and retention bonuses, service expansions, and infrastructure improvements.

All 50 states and the District of Columbia participated in the enhanced FMAP. The total federal investment amounted to approximately \$37 billion, with \$26 billion directed toward workforce-related activities \citep{advancingstates2024}. However, the timing and nature of state spending varied substantially. Some states implemented provider rate increases within months of CMS approval; others took over a year to deploy funds. Some states focused on permanent rate increases; others relied on one-time bonus payments. This variation in implementation, combined with the uniform April 2021 start date for the enhanced FMAP, provides the empirical setting for evaluating ARPA's effectiveness.

The enhanced FMAP expired on March 31, 2022, but states had until March 2024 (later extended to March 2025 for 13 states) to spend accumulated savings. This extended spending window means that ARPA-funded interventions were being implemented throughout the 2021--2024 period, making the post-April 2021 treatment window appropriate for identifying supply-side effects.


\section{Conceptual Framework}

Consider a state $s$ with a Medicaid HCBS provider market. Let $N_{st}$ denote the number of active providers at time $t$, governed by entry and exit flows:
\begin{equation}
N_{st} = N_{s,t-1} + \text{Entry}_{st} - \text{Exit}_{st}
\end{equation}

In steady state, entry roughly equals exit. But if exit accelerates---due to retirements, wage competition, or burnout---and entry does not adjust, the stock $N_{st}$ declines. Define the \emph{pre-period exit rate} $\theta_s$ as the share of providers active in 2018 who are absent from all 2019 billing:
\begin{equation}
\theta_s = \frac{\#\{\text{providers with} \geq 1 \text{ claim in 2018, zero claims in all of 2019}\}}{\#\{\text{providers with} \geq 1 \text{ claim in 2018}\}}
\end{equation}

This measure captures the degree to which the provider network was already eroding before the pandemic arrived. It is \emph{pre-determined} with respect to the pandemic shock---constructed entirely from 2018--2019 billing data, with no reference to post-COVID outcomes. No provider exiting in 2018 or 2019 did so in anticipation of COVID-19.

The pandemic creates a common adverse shock to all provider markets, but its impact is amplified in markets with higher pre-existing depletion. Formally, let $\Delta Y_{st}$ be the change in a service outcome after March 2020. The prediction is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Delta Y_{st}}{\partial \theta_s} < 0
\end{equation}

States with higher $\theta_s$ experience larger declines because they have less network ``slack''---fewer providers to absorb increased demand, cover for absent workers, or maintain service continuity during disruptions.

The prediction applies to multiple outcomes along the supply-access chain. At the supply level, higher $\theta_s$ reduces $N_{st}$ (active providers). But the consequences propagate downstream: with fewer providers, beneficiaries face longer wait times and reduced service availability, leading to declines in $B_{st}$ (beneficiary-provider encounters). Among those who maintain access, the remaining providers may be unable to deliver the same service intensity, potentially reducing claims per beneficiary ($C_{st}/B_{st}$). This supply-access-intensity chain generates testable predictions at each link:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \Delta N_{st}}{\partial \theta_s} < 0, \quad \frac{\partial \Delta B_{st}}{\partial \theta_s} < 0, \quad \frac{\partial \Delta (C_{st}/B_{st})}{\partial \theta_s} \lessgtr 0
\end{equation}
The sign on claims per beneficiary is ambiguous: if the least-served beneficiaries lose access entirely (selection), the average intensity among remaining beneficiaries could rise even as total service delivery falls.

ARPA's enhanced FMAP is modeled as a positive supply shock targeted at HCBS providers. The triple-difference prediction is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial^3 Y_{sjt}}{\partial \text{PostARPA}_t \, \partial \text{HCBS}_j \, \partial \theta_s} > 0
\end{equation}

That is, the ARPA effect on HCBS providers (relative to non-HCBS) is larger in high-$\theta_s$ states, because depleted markets have more ``room'' for recovery and because federal investment has a higher marginal return where provider shortages are most acute.


\section{Data}

\subsection{T-MSIS Medicaid Provider Spending}

The primary dataset is the Transformed Medicaid Statistical Information System (T-MSIS) Provider Spending file, released by the Department of Health and Human Services in February 2026. This dataset represents a breakthrough in Medicaid transparency: it is the first publicly available provider-level claims dataset covering HCBS and behavioral health services.

The data are structured at the billing NPI $\times$ servicing NPI $\times$ HCPCS code $\times$ month level, covering January 2018 through December 2024 (84 months). The primary analysis truncates at June 2024 (78 months) to avoid reporting lag contamination; the full sample is retained for sensitivity. Each observation records the total number of unique beneficiaries, total claims, and total Medicaid payments. The dataset contains approximately 227 million rows, encompassing 617,503 unique billing NPIs and 10,881 unique HCPCS procedure codes. Total recorded payments over the 7-year period sum to \$1.09 trillion.

A critical limitation is that T-MSIS contains \emph{no state identifier}. Geographic attribution relies entirely on joining billing NPIs to the National Plan and Provider Enumeration System (NPPES), which provides each provider's practice state, ZIP code, specialty taxonomy, and enumeration date. The NPPES match rate on billing NPIs is 99.5 percent, and I restrict the sample to providers located in the 50 states plus the District of Columbia.

I classify providers as ``HCBS'' if they bill any T-prefix, H-prefix, or S-prefix HCPCS codes during the pre-treatment period (January 2018 through February 2020) and ``non-HCBS'' if they bill exclusively CPT or other standard medical codes during the same window. This pre-period classification avoids contaminating the group definition with post-treatment billing changes (e.g., providers switching into HCBS codes post-COVID). This classification produces the within-state comparison group for the triple-difference design. Approximately 52 percent of total provider spending flows through HCBS codes, though the number of unique HCBS billing entities is smaller than the non-HCBS count because HCBS providers tend to bill more frequently (monthly recurring services) and at lower per-claim amounts.

\subsection{Pre-COVID Exit Measurement}

The key treatment variable---pre-COVID provider exit intensity---must be measured using only pre-treatment information to avoid contaminating the treatment with the outcome. A natural alternative defines ``exited'' providers as those active in 2018--2019 but absent after February 2020. However, this definition embeds post-treatment information: providers who stopped billing \textit{because of} the pandemic are counted as pre-pandemic exits, creating a mechanical association between the treatment and the outcome.

\textbf{Primary measure (pre-period).} The revised exit rate is constructed as follows. I define a provider as ``active in 2018'' if the billing NPI appears in at least one T-MSIS record during calendar year 2018. I define a provider as ``exited'' if that NPI has \textit{no billing records in any month of 2019}. The state-level exit rate $\theta_s$ is the ratio of exited to active providers:
\begin{equation}
\theta_s = \frac{\#\{\text{NPIs active in 2018, absent in all of 2019}\}_s}{\#\{\text{NPIs active in 2018}\}_s}
\label{eq:exit_rate}
\end{equation}
This measure uses only 2018--2019 data and is therefore free from post-treatment contamination. The mean pre-period exit rate is 16.9 percent (SD = 7.7 pp), with a cross-measure correlation of 0.75 with the pandemic-contaminated definition.

\textbf{Legacy measure (pandemic-contaminated).} For comparison, I also compute the original exit rate definition (active in 2018--2019, absent after February 2020). I report results using both measures, with the pre-period measure as the primary analysis and the legacy measure as a robustness check that reveals the degree of post-treatment contamination.

A provider is classified as ``active'' in a given month if at least one claim appears under that billing NPI. The outcome variable $\ln(\text{active providers})$ uses a $\ln(x+1)$ transformation to handle the small number of state-months with zero active providers of a given type (fewer than 0.5\% of observations).

This exit measure captures departures from the Medicaid billing system during a single pre-treatment transition period (2018 to 2019)---whether due to retirement, practice closure, relocation, or transition to non-Medicaid payers. It is intentionally broad: from the perspective of Medicaid beneficiaries, any reason for a provider's departure reduces access to care.

The measure aggregates heterogeneous exit decisions into a single state-level rate. Some exits reflect individual career decisions; others reflect organizational decisions (agency closure, merger). I test for differential effects by NPI Entity Type in the robustness analysis (Section 6.6), finding no significant difference between individual (Type 1) and organizational (Type 2) providers.

The pre-period exit rate varies substantially across states, ranging from approximately 10 to 55 percent of the 2018 active provider base. This variation is driven by reimbursement adequacy, workforce demographics, state regulatory environment, and the structure of HCBS delivery. The key empirical question is whether this variation predicts differential pandemic outcomes, conditional on state and time fixed effects.

\subsection{Shift-Share Instrument}

To address concerns that the pre-COVID exit rate reflects unobserved state characteristics correlated with pandemic outcomes, I construct a Bartik-style shift-share instrument following \citet{goldsmithpinkham2020}. The instrument uses:
\begin{equation}
Z_s = \sum_k \text{share}_{sk}^{2018} \times \text{national\_exit}_k
\end{equation}
where $\text{share}_{sk}^{2018}$ is the share of state $s$'s Medicaid providers in specialty $k$ (HCBS, behavioral health, physician, other) as of 2018, and $\text{national\_exit}_k$ is the national 2018-to-2019 exit rate for specialty $k$ excluding state $s$. Both components now use only pre-treatment information, consistent with the revised exit rate definition. The instrument isolates variation in exit rates driven by the interaction of states' pre-existing specialty composition with national trends in specialty-specific attrition.

\subsection{Additional Data Sources}

I supplement T-MSIS with several external datasets:

\textbf{Census ACS 5-Year (2019):} State-level demographics including population, median household income, poverty rate, racial composition, and median age. These serve as pre-treatment controls.

\textbf{FRED:} Monthly state unemployment rates from the Federal Reserve Economic Data system, used as a time-varying control for local labor market conditions that affect both provider exit and health outcomes.

\textbf{CDC Provisional COVID-19 Deaths:} Weekly state-level COVID-19 and total deaths from the National Center for Health Statistics, aggregated to monthly frequency and normalized per 100,000 population. Used in mediation analysis and vulnerability interaction specifications (see Section 5.4).

\textbf{Oxford COVID-19 Government Response Tracker (OxCGRT):} Daily state-level policy stringency indices, aggregated to monthly averages. Used as a control for the intensity of lockdown measures.

\section{Empirical Strategy}

\subsection{Part 1: Pre-COVID Exits and Pandemic Disruption}

The first analysis examines whether states with higher pre-pandemic provider exit rates experienced larger service disruptions after March 2020. I estimate a continuous-treatment event study:
\begin{equation}
Y_{st} = \alpha_s + \delta_t + \sum_{k \neq -1} \beta_k \left( \theta_s \times \ind[t = k] \right) + X_{st} \gamma + \varepsilon_{st}
\label{eq:eventstudy}
\end{equation}
where $Y_{st}$ is a log outcome (active HCBS providers, beneficiary-provider encounters, total claims) in state $s$ and month $t$; $\alpha_s$ and $\delta_t$ are state and month fixed effects; $\theta_s$ is the pre-COVID exit rate; $\ind[t = k]$ are monthly event-time indicators relative to March 2020 ($k = 0$); and $X_{st}$ includes state unemployment and COVID stringency. Standard errors are clustered at the state level.

Note that because the treatment variable $\theta_s$ is continuous and time-invariant (measured once, pre-pandemic), the concerns about staggered adoption and negative weighting highlighted by \citet{goodmanbacon2021} and \citet{callaway2021} do not apply: there is no variation in treatment timing, and all treated units are ``always treated'' at different intensities. The coefficients $\beta_k$ trace out the dynamic effect of a one-unit increase in exit intensity on outcomes. The key identifying assumption is that, absent the pandemic, outcomes in high-exit and low-exit states would have evolved in parallel. I test this by examining the pre-treatment coefficients ($\beta_k$ for $k < 0$), which should be jointly insignificant and individually close to zero if the parallel trends assumption holds.

For the static (collapsed) specification, I estimate:
\begin{equation}
Y_{st} = \alpha_s + \delta_t + \beta \left( \theta_s \times \text{Post}_{t} \right) + X_{st} \gamma + \varepsilon_{st}
\label{eq:did}
\end{equation}
where $\text{Post}_t = \ind[t \geq \text{March 2020}]$ and $\beta$ captures the average post-pandemic differential effect per unit of exit intensity.

\subsection{Broken-Trend Model}

Given the possibility of pre-existing differential trends between high- and low-attrition states, the broken-trend specification is the key test in this revision. It allows an ongoing differential trend identified from the pre-period, separates this from any discrete level shift at COVID onset, and asks whether the post-COVID trajectory \textit{accelerates} relative to the pre-trend:
\begin{equation}
Y_{st} = \alpha_s + \delta_t + \lambda \left( \theta_s \times t \right) + \beta \left( \theta_s \times \text{Post}_{t} \right) + \kappa \left( \theta_s \times \text{Post}_{t} \times t \right) + X_{st} \gamma + \varepsilon_{st}
\label{eq:broken_trend}
\end{equation}
where $t$ is a dense-ranked month index (0, 1, 2, \ldots). The coefficient $\lambda$ captures the ongoing differential trend (identified from pre-period variation); $\beta$ captures the level shift at COVID onset; and $\kappa$ captures the slope change post-COVID. The key parameter is $\kappa$: a negative $\kappa$ would indicate pandemic acceleration of the pre-existing differential decline; a positive $\kappa$ would indicate moderation or reversal. Month fixed effects $\delta_t$ absorb the common time path, so the interactions are identified from cross-state variation in $\theta_s$ within each month.

\subsection{Collapsed Cross-Sectional Regression}

To complement the panel specification, I estimate a simple collapsed cross-sectional regression with $N = 51$ observations (one per state):
\begin{equation}
\Delta \overline{Y}_s = \alpha + \beta \theta_s + u_s
\label{eq:collapsed}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta \overline{Y}_s = \overline{Y}_{s,\text{post}} - \overline{Y}_{s,\text{pre}}$ is the change in mean log providers between the post-COVID and pre-COVID periods. This avoids serial correlation concerns and directly tests whether higher pre-period attrition predicts larger pandemic-era declines. Standard errors use HC2 and HC3 heteroskedasticity-robust estimators.

\subsection{Instrumental Variable Estimation}

To address concerns that the exit rate may reflect unobserved state characteristics, I construct a Bartik-style shift-share instrument $Z_s$ (defined in Section 4.3) and estimate the reduced form:
\begin{equation}
Y_{st} = \alpha_s + \delta_t + \pi \left( Z_s \times \text{Post}_{t} \right) + X_{st} \gamma + \varepsilon_{st}
\end{equation}
The coefficient $\hat{\pi}$ captures the ``intent-to-treat'' effect of predicted exit exposure on provider supply. The first-stage F-statistic is 7.5, below the conventional threshold of 10 for strong instruments, making the IV uninformative for this application. I retain the reduced-form specification as a descriptive check: the reduced-form coefficient is negative and directionally consistent with the OLS, though imprecise ($t = 1.64$). The Anderson-Rubin confidence set, which is robust to weak instruments, is reported in the sensitivity analysis (Section 6.8) and includes zero ($[-7.64, 2.36]$), consistent with the null finding. Given the weak first stage, the IV does not provide independent evidence for or against a causal interpretation.

\subsection{Part 2: ARPA Recovery (Triple-Difference)}

The second analysis is more exploratory, examining whether ARPA's uniform HCBS investment differentially benefited depleted markets. Because ARPA was implemented simultaneously in all 50 states and DC at the same enhanced FMAP rate, there is no cross-state variation in treatment intensity---only variation in the pre-existing ``dose'' of depletion. The DDD therefore estimates the interaction of ARPA timing, provider type, and pre-existing exit intensity:
\begin{equation}
Y_{sjt} = \alpha_{sj} + \delta_{jt} + \beta \, P_{t} H_{j} D_s + X_{st} \gamma + \varepsilon_{sjt}
\label{eq:ddd}
\end{equation}
where $s$ indexes states, $j \in \{\text{HCBS}, \text{Non-HCBS}\}$ indexes provider type, $t$ indexes months; $P_t = \ind[t \geq \text{April 2021}]$; $H_j = \ind[j = \text{HCBS}]$; and $D_s = \ind[\theta_s > \text{median}]$. The specification includes state$\times$provider-type fixed effects ($\alpha_{sj}$) and provider-type$\times$month fixed effects ($\delta_{jt}$). The lower-order two-way interactions ($P_t H_j$, $P_t D_s$, $H_j D_s$) are included as regressors; some are partially absorbed by the fixed effects but are retained in the estimating equation for completeness.

The coefficient of interest is $\beta$: the differential post-ARPA change in HCBS provider outcomes in high-exit states, relative to non-HCBS providers in the same states and HCBS providers in low-exit states. The state$\times$type fixed effects absorb time-invariant differences between groups, while the type$\times$month fixed effects absorb common time trends specific to each provider type.

I also estimate a continuous version replacing the binary $D_s$ with the continuous exit rate $\theta_s$, and a dynamic version with quarterly event-time indicators.

\subsection{Threats to Validity}

\textbf{Selection into exit intensity.} High-exit states may differ from low-exit states along dimensions correlated with pandemic outcomes (poverty, rural share, healthcare infrastructure). I address this through: (1) extensive pre-treatment controls; (2) state fixed effects that absorb all time-invariant state characteristics; (3) the shift-share IV that isolates variation driven by national specialty trends; and (4) pre-trend tests showing no differential outcomes before the pandemic.

\textbf{Concurrent policies.} COVID lockdowns, federal relief funds, Medicaid eligibility changes, and labor market disruptions all occurred simultaneously. I control for state-level COVID stringency (OxCGRT), unemployment (FRED), and COVID mortality (CDC). The within-state DDD comparison for ARPA further absorbs state-level confounders.

\textbf{Measurement of exits.} Not all billing cessation represents retirement. Providers may stop billing Medicaid while continuing to practice for private payers. This measurement error would attenuate results, biasing estimates toward zero. The NPPES enumeration date provides a partial check: exit rates are substantially higher among long-tenured providers, consistent with retirement as a primary driver.

\textbf{Inference with 51 clusters.} With 51 state-level clusters, standard clustered standard errors are reliable. I supplement with randomization inference (2,000 permutations) as described in Section 6.7.

\subsection{Identification Challenge: COVID Severity as Mediator}

A natural instinct is to control for COVID deaths per capita, reasoning that pandemic severity is a confounder: states hit harder by COVID experienced both more deaths and more healthcare disruption. But the causal structure is more subtle. Consider the directed acyclic graph (DAG):

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}[
  node distance=2.5cm,
  every node/.style={draw, rounded corners, minimum height=1cm, minimum width=2cm, align=center, font=\small},
  arrow/.style={->, thick, >=stealth}
]
  \node (theta) {$\theta_s$\\Pre-COVID\\Exit Rate};
  \node (Y) [right=3.5cm of theta] {$Y_{st}$\\Provider Supply/\\Beneficiary Access};
  \node (covid) [below right=1.5cm and 1cm of theta] {COVID\\Severity};
  \node (U) [above=1.2cm of covid] {$U_s$\\State Characteristics};

  \draw[arrow] (theta) -- (Y) node[midway, above, draw=none, minimum height=0pt, minimum width=0pt] {\small direct};
  \draw[arrow] (theta) -- (covid) node[midway, left, draw=none, minimum height=0pt, minimum width=0pt] {\small mediator};
  \draw[arrow] (covid) -- (Y);
  \draw[arrow, dashed] (U) -- (theta);
  \draw[arrow, dashed] (U) -- (covid);
  \draw[arrow, dashed] (U) -- (Y);
\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

COVID severity plays a dual role. It is partly a \textbf{confounder}: unobserved state characteristics $U_s$ (population density, healthcare infrastructure, political attitudes toward lockdowns) affect both exit rates and pandemic severity. But it is also partly a \textbf{mediator}: depleted provider networks may have worsened pandemic outcomes---patients in areas with fewer HCBS providers had reduced access to care, potentially increasing hospitalizations and deaths.

Following \citet{angrist2009} and \citet{pearl2009}, conditioning on a mediator biases the estimate of the total effect toward zero. If $\theta_s \to \text{COVID severity} \to Y_{st}$ is an active causal pathway, then controlling for COVID deaths ``shuts off'' this channel and yields only the direct effect of depletion net of pandemic severity---understating the full association between depletion and outcomes.

I take an empirical approach to this identification challenge. The \textbf{main specification} (equation \ref{eq:did}) omits COVID severity controls, estimating the \textbf{total effect} of pre-COVID exit intensity on post-pandemic outcomes. I then present two additional specifications:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Specification 2:} Adds COVID deaths per capita. If the coefficient barely changes, then COVID severity is not an important confounder (and may primarily operate as a mediator).
\item \textbf{Specification 3:} Adds COVID deaths and government stringency. This estimates the direct effect conditional on both pandemic severity and policy response.
\end{itemize}

Comparing coefficients across these specifications is informative. A large drop from Specification 1 to 2 would suggest confounding; stability would suggest the total and direct effects are similar---either because confounding is minimal or because the mediating channel exactly offsets confounding. I also estimate a \textbf{vulnerability interaction} ($\theta_s \times \text{COVID deaths}_{st}$) to test whether depleted states experienced disproportionately worse outcomes when hit by severe COVID---a test of the amplification mechanism that does not require taking a stand on the mediator question.

\subsection{Formal Sensitivity to Pre-Trend Violations}

The pre-period exit rate generates pre-trends by construction: states losing more providers in the 2018-to-2019 transition were mechanically experiencing declining provider counts during the pre-treatment period. I apply the \citet{rambachan2023} framework and the augmented synthetic control of \citet{benmichael2021}. Both analyses are consistent with the null: HonestDiD yields a breakdown $\bar{M} = 0$ (the result does not survive even strict parallel trends), and the augsynth average ATT is 0.128 (positive, not negative as the original hypothesis predicted; no permutation-based $p$-value is available for this specification). These diagnostics, which previously generated ``tension'' with significant results under the contaminated measure, are now fully consistent with the static DiD null.

The pre-COVID trend shows a gradual decline, and the March 2020 drop is sharply discontinuous. \textbf{Synthesis.} The robustness analysis is unambiguous. Using a purely pre-treatment exit rate, no specification---static DiD, broken-trend, collapsed cross-sectional, randomization inference under five stratifications, wild cluster bootstrap, HonestDiD, or augmented synthetic control---produces a significant association between pre-period provider attrition and pandemic-era disruption. The sharp contrast with the pandemic-contaminated measure ($-0.844$, $p = 0.017$) confirms that the significance of the contaminated measure is an artifact of post-treatment contamination, not a genuine causal effect.


\section{Discussion}

\subsection{What the Null Tells Us}

The central finding of this revision is a null: a purely pre-treatment measure of provider attrition does not predict pandemic-era service disruption. This null is informative in three ways.

First, it resolves a potential identification puzzle. When exit rates are measured using post-treatment absence, three of four sensitivity checks (HonestDiD, augsynth, state trends) fail, while conditional RI within Census divisions ($p = 0.038$) appears to support the finding. The resolution is simple: the exit rate was contaminated by post-treatment information. Once the contamination is removed, \textit{all} diagnostics agree---there is no significant effect.

Second, the broken-trend model reveals the underlying process. High-attrition states were on declining provider trajectories before COVID ($\lambda = -0.029$, $p = 0.025$). This ongoing erosion is the substantive finding: the HCBS safety net was fraying well before the pandemic. But the pandemic did not accelerate the decline---if anything, the post-COVID trajectory improved ($\kappa = 0.033$, $p = 0.024$), perhaps because federal intervention (ARPA's \$37 billion) partially arrested the pre-existing decline.

Third, the comparison between exit rate definitions provides a methodological cautionary tale. Administrative claims data measure billing activity, not workforce status. Defining ``exits'' using post-treatment absence---a seemingly natural approach for measuring pre-pandemic attrition---embeds the pandemic shock into the treatment variable. The resulting mechanical association is indistinguishable from a causal effect in standard DiD specifications. This measurement problem is not unique to Medicaid: any study measuring attrition in administrative data by observing future absence faces the same risk.

\subsection{The Pre-COVID Decline: Descriptive Patterns}

The broken-trend model's significant $\lambda$ documents a pre-COVID decline in HCBS provider capacity that warrants attention independent of the pandemic. States with higher 2018-to-2019 attrition had steeper provider count trajectories throughout the sample. This is consistent with the \citet{hirschman1970} exit dynamic: once provider networks begin thinning, the process is self-reinforcing. Remaining providers face heavier caseloads, longer travel times, and reduced peer support, which accelerates further exit. Whether this process constitutes hysteresis in the \citet{blanchardsummers1986} sense---a temporary shock permanently altering the equilibrium---cannot be determined from these data, because the ``shock'' that initiated the decline is unobserved. The data show only that the decline was ongoing and persistent.

\subsection{From Providers to People}

The beneficiary-side results mirror the provider null: the pre-period exit rate does not predict differential changes in beneficiary-provider encounters ($0.492$, $p = 0.58$). The broken-trend model shows a directionally similar pattern (negative pre-trend, positive post-slope) but coefficients are imprecise. The data do not support a ``multiplier'' interpretation---where each lost provider severs access for multiple beneficiaries. The ongoing pre-COVID decline does affect beneficiaries, but this is a level effect captured by the pre-trend $\lambda$, not a pandemic-specific acceleration.

These findings reframe the policy question posed by the Oregon Health Insurance Experiment \citep{finkelstein2012,baicker2013}. The relevant constraint in HCBS is supply, not coverage. But the revision clarifies that the pandemic did not create an additional supply shock beyond the pre-existing decline. The workforce erosion documented here is chronic, not acute.

\subsection{ARPA and Post-COVID Recovery}

The positive $\kappa$ in the broken-trend model admits a speculative interpretation: ARPA's \$37 billion HCBS investment, beginning in April 2021, may have disproportionately benefited the most depleted markets. The DDD estimates remain imprecise---all states adopted ARPA simultaneously, limiting within-variation---but the broken-trend slope improvement is consistent with policy effectiveness. A more targeted analysis with state-level ARPA spending intensity data (unavailable in the current dataset) could test this hypothesis directly.

\subsection{Limitations}

Four limitations warrant emphasis. First, T-MSIS measures billing activity, not workforce headcount. A provider who stops billing Medicaid may continue working for private-pay clients. The exit measure captures Medicaid-specific access loss, not total workforce depletion---though this is the margin relevant for Medicaid beneficiaries.

Second, the state-level unit of analysis masks within-state heterogeneity. County- or ZIP-level analysis would be ideal but is constrained by T-MSIS's suppression of cells with fewer than 12 claims.

Third, the pre-period exit rate (2018-to-2019) captures a single transition window. A state's exit rate in this window may not represent its long-run attrition propensity. A multi-year pre-treatment exit rate would strengthen the measure but requires data extending before 2018, which T-MSIS does not provide.

Fourth, the null finding on pandemic amplification does not mean the HCBS workforce is healthy. The significant pre-trend ($\lambda < 0$) documents ongoing erosion. The null pertains specifically to whether the pandemic \textit{accelerated} this erosion beyond trend---it did not---but the underlying decline is real and policy-relevant.


\section{Conclusion}

This paper began with a simple question---does pre-pandemic provider attrition predict pandemic-era disruption?---and arrived at a more interesting answer than anticipated. Using a purely pre-treatment exit rate (2018-active, absent in all 2019 billing), the predictive association that motivated earlier versions of this paper disappears entirely. The static DiD, collapsed cross-sectional, randomization inference, and augmented synthetic control all yield nulls. But the broken-trend model reveals what the static specification obscures: an ongoing pre-COVID decline in HCBS provider capacity in high-attrition states ($\lambda < 0$, $p = 0.025$), no pandemic-specific level shift ($\beta \approx 0$), and post-COVID trajectory improvement ($\kappa > 0$, $p = 0.024$).

Two contributions emerge. The \textbf{substantive contribution} is documenting the pre-COVID erosion of the HCBS safety net. States where more providers left the Medicaid billing system between 2018 and 2019 were on steeper decline trajectories throughout the subsequent six years. This ongoing attrition---affecting the personal care aides, behavioral health counselors, and home health workers who deliver hands-on care to approximately 5 million elderly and disabled Americans---warrants policy attention regardless of the pandemic. The broken-trend model's positive $\kappa$ offers cautious optimism: the post-COVID trajectory improved, possibly reflecting ARPA's investment, but the level of provider capacity in high-attrition states remains below where it would be absent the pre-existing decline.

The \textbf{methodological contribution} is a cautionary tale about post-treatment contamination in administrative exit rate measures. The pandemic-contaminated definition (active in 2018--2019, absent after February 2020) yields a significant coefficient of $-0.844$; the purely pre-treatment definition yields $+0.614$. The sign reversal is dramatic and consequential: the causal narrative that ``depletion causes pandemic fragility'' is an artifact of embedding the outcome into the treatment. This measurement problem is not unique to Medicaid. Any study measuring attrition in administrative data by observing future absence risks the same contamination. The lesson is simple: define treatment using only pre-treatment information, or the results cannot be trusted.

The policy conclusion is nuanced. The HCBS workforce was eroding before COVID, and this erosion is real and harmful. But the pandemic did not amplify the decline, and ARPA may have partially arrested it. The approximately 5 million Medicaid beneficiaries who depend on home-based care need sustained investment in workforce capacity---competitive and permanent reimbursement rates, meaningful career pathways, institutional support---but the rationale is the chronic pre-existing decline, not acute pandemic-induced fragility.

\section{Data Appendix}

\subsection{T-MSIS Data Processing}

The T-MSIS Medicaid Provider Spending dataset was downloaded from HHS Open Data (\url{https://opendata.hhs.gov/datasets/medicaid-provider-spending/}) in Parquet format. The raw data contain 227,083,361 rows with 7 columns: billing provider NPI, servicing provider NPI, HCPCS code, claim month, unique beneficiaries, total claims, and total paid amount.

The raw data cover January 2018 through December 2024 (84 months). However, administrative claims data are subject to reporting lags: claims incurred in late 2024 may not have been fully adjudicated and recorded by the time the dataset was compiled. The primary analysis truncates the sample at June 2024 (78 months) to avoid reporting lag contamination; the full sample through December 2024 is retained for sensitivity analysis.

Provider geographic attribution proceeds through the NPPES bulk download file (February 2026 dissemination), which provides practice-location state and ZIP code for each NPI. The match rate between T-MSIS billing NPIs and NPPES records is 99.5 percent. I restrict the sample to providers with practice locations in the 50 U.S.\ states plus the District of Columbia.

HCPCS codes are classified by prefix: T-codes (home and community-based services), H-codes (behavioral health), S-codes (temporary/state Medicaid services), and all others (CPT, J-codes, A-codes, etc.). A provider is classified as ``HCBS'' if they bill any T/H/S code during the pre-treatment period (January 2018 through February 2020), and ``Non-HCBS'' otherwise. This pre-period classification ensures group membership is not contaminated by post-treatment billing changes.

\subsection{Exit Rate Construction}

\textbf{Primary measure (pre-period).} The pre-period exit rate is computed as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
\item Identify all billing NPIs with at least one claim in calendar year 2018 (``active in 2018'').
\item Among these, identify NPIs with no claims in any month of calendar year 2019 (``exited'').
\item Compute the state-level exit rate as the ratio of exited to active providers.
\end{enumerate}
This measure uses only 2018--2019 data and is free from post-treatment contamination.

\textbf{Legacy measure (pandemic-contaminated).} For robustness comparison, I also compute the original exit rate: NPIs active in 2018--2019 but absent after February 2020. As documented in the main text, this measure embeds post-treatment information and generates a spurious association with pandemic outcomes.

\subsection{Shift-Share Instrument}

The Bartik instrument follows \citet{goldsmithpinkham2020}. Provider specialties are classified into four categories using NPPES taxonomy codes: (1) HCBS (taxonomy codes associated with personal care, home health, and residential services); (2) behavioral health (taxonomy prefixes 101Y, 103T, 106H, 2084P, 363L); (3) physician (taxonomy prefixes 207, 208, 174); and (4) other. The instrument is the predicted exit rate from interacting state-level baseline (2018) specialty shares with leave-one-out national specialty-specific 2018-to-2019 exit rates, consistent with the pre-period exit rate definition.
