## Launch Prompt

> write a paper on this: Many online platforms distribute new content in discrete chronological batches with hard cutoffs (daily/weekly "new" lists, email digests, app-store review queues, preprint announcements). Items that arrive just before the cutoff typically receive substantially more early visibility (top-of-list placement, inclusion in the next digest, longer time on the front page, more favorable day-of-week exposure) than items arriving just after, even when underlying quality is similar within a narrow time window. This creates quasi-random variation in early attention that can be used as an instrument for attention or early diffusion.
>
> A canonical setting is arXiv's daily submission/announcement cycle. Papers submitted shortly before the daily cutoff appear in the next announcement (often near the top of their category list), while papers submitted shortly after wait an additional cycle and face a different competitive set and timing. Define the instrument as an indicator for being submitted within a narrow window before versus after the cutoff, or as the predicted announcement position implied by submission time. The first stage is the effect of this cutoff-based assignment on early attention measures such as downloads, pageviews, mentions on social media, newsletter/blog coverage, or early citations. The second stage estimates the causal effect of attention on longer-run outcomes such as cumulative citations, replication/uptake by other papers, topic diffusion, author career outcomes, funding, or downstream innovation.
>
> Identification relies on local randomization around the cutoff: within a tight bandwidth (e.g., minutes to an hour), which side of the cutoff a submission lands on is plausibly as-good-as-random, but it deterministically shifts visibility. Key diagnostics include manipulation tests for bunching around the cutoff, balance tests on pre-determined paper/author characteristics, and event-study style validation that only attention jumps at the cutoff. Robustness comes from varying bandwidths, excluding periods with known strategic timing (e.g., major conference deadlines), controlling flexibly for time-of-day and day-of-week, and using placebos (fake cutoffs or categories where ordering is not driven by submission time). The template generalizes beyond arXiv to any setting with batching or chronological ranking, enabling a scalable, repeated quasi-experiment for studying the economic returns to attention and visibility.
