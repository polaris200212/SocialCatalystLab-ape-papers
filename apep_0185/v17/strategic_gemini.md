## 1. THE ELEVATOR PITCH
The paper asks if minimum wage increases in one state (e.g., California) propagate to workers in distant, low-wage states (e.g., Texas) through social networks rather than policy imitation. By linking Facebook’s Social Connectedness Index with population data, the authors find that workers in highly connected counties update their labor market behavior based on distant wage signals, leading to higher local earnings and employment. This is a first-order question because it challenges the "local" nature of labor markets and suggests that information, not just migration, is a key driver of spatial equilibrium.

**Evaluation:** The paper articulates this pitch effectively. The first two paragraphs successfully use the "El Paso vs. Amarillo" anecdote to ground a complex network problem in intuitive geography. The current opening is strong, but to make it "AER-grade," the second paragraph should emphasize the shift from *shares* to *scale* (population weighting) even earlier, as this is the paper’s primary methodological contribution.

---

## 2. CONTRIBUTION CLARITY
The paper identifies a large, positive spillover effect of distant minimum wage increases on local labor markets, driven by social network-mediated information rather than physical migration.

**Evaluation:**
- **Differentiation:** It differentiates itself from Dube et al. (2014) by looking at social rather than geographic proximity, and from Bailey et al. by introducing "population weighting" as a necessary correction for SCI-based shift-share designs.
- **Framing:** It is framed as a question about the world (how do workers learn about their value?), which is a strength.
- **"What's New":** A smart economist would say: "It's the paper that proves California's minimum wage hikes effectively raised reservation wages in Texas through Facebook 'friends'."
- **Growth:** To make the contribution "bigger," the paper could benefit from more direct evidence on the *search* side. While job flows are a good proxy, even a small-scale survey or supplemental data on "reservation wage" search terms from Google Trends in these specific counties would bridge the gap between "macro result" and "micro mechanism."

---

## 3. LITERATURE POSITIONING
The paper sits at the intersection of Labor (Minimum Wage), Urban (Spatial Spillovers), and the emerging "Facebook/SCI" literature.

- **Neighbors:** Dube, Lester, and Reich (2010/2014); Bailey et al. (2018); Jäger et al. (2024); Moretti (2011).
- **Positioning:** It **builds on** Bailey by refining the tool and **synthesizes** the minimum wage literature with the "worker misperceptions" literature (Jäger et al.). 
- **Right Conversation?** Yes. It avoids the tired "does the minimum wage kill jobs?" debate and instead asks how policy in one place changes the *information set* of workers in another. This is a much more "AER" way to approach the topic.

---

## 4. NARRATIVE ARC
- **Setup:** Labor markets are traditionally seen as local or linked by migration.
- **Tension:** Policy divergence (Fight for $15) created huge wage gaps, but we don't know if these gaps matter to workers who stay put.
- **Resolution:** They do. Social networks transmit "wage news" that increases worker bargaining power and search intensity locally.
- **Implications:** Policy evaluation must account for network externalities; the "scale" of connections is the critical unit of measurement.

**Evaluation:** The arc is exceptionally clear. The distinction between "probability-weighting" (null) and "population-weighting" (strong results) provides the "aha!" moment that top journals crave.

---

## 5. THE "SO WHAT?" TEST
**The Party Fact:** "When California raises its minimum wage, employment in El Paso goes up by 9% because workers find out about it on Facebook and start demanding more from their local bosses."

**Reaction:** People will lean in. The 9% employment figure is eye-popping (perhaps suspiciously so) and demands an explanation of the mechanism. The "So What" is that the "effective" labor supply is nationalized by social media even if the physical labor supply remains local.

---

## 6. STRUCTURAL SUGGESTIONS
- **The "Scale vs. Share" argument:** This is the most interesting technical contribution. It should be moved even earlier. 
- **Migration/Job Flows:** These sections (Section 9) are the most critical for defending the "Information Channel." They should be expanded with more robustness tests (e.g., heterogeneous effects by age, assuming younger workers are more Facebook-active).
- **Appendix:** The formal model (Appendix A) is simple and elegant; it could arguably be moved into the main body to give the "Information Channel" more theoretical weight.

---

## 7. WHAT WOULD MAKE THIS AN AER PAPER?
The distance between this and a "Yes" is **the magnitude of the employment effect.** A 9% increase in employment from a $1 change in *distant* network wages is massive—larger than many *local* multipliers. 

**Single Most Impactful Advice:** The authors must "stress-test the magnitude." They need to provide a "common sense" calibration. If the 9% is a LATE for high-complier counties, they need to show exactly who those workers are and if it's plausible that information alone can move 9% of a county's workforce into (or between) jobs. If the magnitude holds up to a rigorous "economic plausibility" check, it’s a slam dunk.
