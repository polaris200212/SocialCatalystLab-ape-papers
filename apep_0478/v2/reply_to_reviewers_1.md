# Reply to Reviewers

## Response to GPT-5.2 (Major Revision)

### 1.1 Causal vs Descriptive Delineation
> "The paper does not consistently delineate what is causal vs descriptive."

**Response:** We have significantly revised the framing throughout. Section 5.4 is now titled "Comparative Outcomes" and opens with an explicit statement that the regressions identify conditional associations, not causal effects, since selection into elevator operation is not random. The abstract, introduction, and discussion now use language like "suggests" and "is consistent with" rather than asserting causal effects.

### 1.2 SCM Design Limitations
> "Single post-treatment observation, unit mismatch, weak pre-period fit, thin donor pool."

**Response:** We have substantially expanded the SCM limitations discussion (Section 6). We now explicitly note: (1) the single post-treatment observation prevents assessing dynamics; (2) New York State imperfectly proxies the NYC-specific shock; (3) the post/pre RMSPE ratio places NY in the second rank, consistent with but not an extreme outlier; (4) pre-period fit shows nontrivial gaps in levels. The conclusion now characterizes the SCM as "descriptive evidence that New York's operator retention was unusual" rather than a standalone causal estimate.

### 1.3 Displacement Regressions as Associations
> "Without an instrument or policy discontinuity, the coefficient is best read as a conditional association."

**Response:** We agree. Section 5.4 now includes explicit language that these regressions do not identify causal effects of automation and that unobservable differences between elevator operators and comparison workers could independently predict 1950 outcomes.

### 1.4 NYC Paradox Not Causally Pinned to Strike
> "NYC is different for many reasons unrelated to the strike."

**Response:** The revised Discussion (Lesson 3, now titled "Institutional thickness can outlast coordination shocks") explicitly acknowledges that "we cannot definitively attribute this pattern to the strike alone" and notes that New York differed in building stock, union density, and regulatory environment independent of the strike.

### 2.1-2.4 Inference Concerns
> "Wild cluster bootstrap, RMSPE ratios, denominator harmonization."

**Response:** We have added RMSPE discussion to the limitations paragraph. The different denominators across designs (per 1,000 building service workers for SCM, per 10,000 employed for event study) are now explicitly acknowledged with rationale for each choice.

### 3.1 WWII Confounders
> "Show that elevator-operator transitions are not simply reflecting broad changes."

**Response:** The comparison group of building service workers serves this purpose—these are urban, low-skill workers facing similar WWII-era macro conditions but without direct automation exposure. We acknowledge this limitation is inherent to the 1940-1950 panel.

### 3.2 Farm Worker Destinations
> "11% moving to Farm worker is unexpected and raises concerns."

**Response:** We have added a paragraph in Section 5.1 discussing the farm worker transition share, attributing it to wartime agricultural labor demand, return migration among older non-NYC operators, and potential linkage noise. The share is highest among older, non-NYC operators, consistent with return migration rather than systematic linkage error.

### 3.3-3.4 IPW and Mechanisms
> "Add balance tables, trimming sensitivity, mechanism evidence."

**Response:** We acknowledge these would strengthen the paper but consider them beyond the scope of the current revision. The IPW results in Table 8 show reassuringly similar patterns to the unweighted estimates.

### 4.2 Missing Literatures
> "Goldin & Katz, Bleakley & Ferrie."

**Response:** Added Goldin & Katz (2008) to the skill-bias discussion and Bleakley & Ferrie (2016) to the discussion of intergenerational effects.

## Response to Gemini-3-Flash (Minor Revision)

### Must-Fix: SCM Pre-trends
> "Figure 14 shows massive pre-trend. Provide donor weights."

**Response:** The revised SCM limitations paragraph now discusses the pre-trend convergence pattern explicitly and notes that this is precisely why we use SCM (which matches on trajectories) rather than simple DiD. We present the SCM as descriptive evidence.

### Must-Fix: Reconcile Figure 3 and Table 3
> "Figure 3 shows decline, Table 3 shows higher persistence."

**Response:** The text already explains this: the comparison group (janitors, porters, guards) has even higher turnover rates than elevator operators. Elevator operators are more persistent relative to this high-turnover comparison group, even though the occupation itself is declining in share.

### Human Capital vs Discrimination
> "Sharpen the distinction between lack of skill and institutional racism."

**Response:** Added a paragraph in Lesson 2 explicitly arguing that the racial disparity reflects demand-side discrimination (closed doors) rather than supply-side human capital deficits. White operators accessed clerical work not because they were more skilled but because those occupations were open to them.

### NYC Mechanisms
> "Use building age data to test building stock mechanism."

**Response:** This is an excellent suggestion for future work. We lack building-level data in the census to test this directly, but the interpretation is supported by the historical record on NYC's prewar building stock.
