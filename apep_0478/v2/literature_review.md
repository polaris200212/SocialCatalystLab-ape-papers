# Early Literature Review (Revision of apep_0478 v1)

## Overlap Assessment: LOW

This paper occupies a unique niche at the intersection of automation history, labor economics, and urban economic history. The elevator operator case study has no direct academic antecedent.

### Key Related Human Papers

1. **Autor, Levy, and Murnane (2003)** — "The Skill Content of Recent Technological Change" — Foundational routine-task framework. Our contribution: historical micro-level test of the routine-replacement hypothesis.

2. **Akcomak, Kok, and Rojas (2016)** — "Technology and the Decline of the Elevator Operator" — Most directly related but uses aggregate trends only. Our contribution: individual-level linked panel showing occupational transitions.

3. **Abramitzky, Boustan, and Eriksson (2014, 2019, 2021)** — Census linking methodology. We use their MLP v2.0 crosswalk as infrastructure.

4. **Abadie, Diamond, and Hainmueller (2010, 2015)** — SCM methodology applied in our aggregate analysis.

### Delta Statement

No existing paper examines individual-level occupational transitions of workers displaced by a specific historical automation technology using linked census microdata. The "Paradox of the Epicenter" — that NYC operators persisted longer despite being the locus of automation — is entirely novel.
