# Early Literature Review: apep_0483 v2

## Key Human Papers

1. **Britton & Propper (2016)** "Teacher Pay and School Productivity" — JPubE. Most directly related. Uses England STPCD setting but cross-sectional. Our panel design extends this with within-LA variation.
   - Overlap risk: Medium
   - Delta: We use panel FE and Progress 8 (value-added); they use cross-sectional variation and raw achievement.

2. **Hendricks (2014)** "The Effect of the Teacher Pay Premium" — JLE. Florida setting. Finds pay premium affects achievement, concentrated among disadvantaged. Our FSM heterogeneity echoes this finding.
   - Overlap risk: Low (different country, different institutional setting)
   - Delta: England's nationally set STPCD provides cleaner institutional variation than Florida's district-set pay.

3. **Sims (2020)** "What Happens When You Pay Teachers More?" — Oxford Rev Educ. England setting, examines austerity-era pay restraint and teacher supply. Focused on supply, not student outcomes.
   - Overlap risk: Medium
   - Delta: We link competitiveness to student outcomes (Progress 8), not just teacher supply.

4. **Dolton & Marcenaro-Gutierrez (2011)** "If You Pay Peanuts" — Econ Policy. Cross-country analysis of teacher pay and performance. Macro-level, not micro.
   - Overlap risk: Low
   - Delta: Within-country, within-LA variation; value-added outcomes; mechanism evidence.

5. **Clotfelter et al. (2008)** "Would Higher Salaries Keep Teachers in High-Poverty Schools?" — JPubE. North Carolina bonus program. RCT-like design.
   - Overlap risk: Low (different country, different policy)
   - Delta: England's STPCD constraint operates through relative wage compression, not targeted bonuses.

## Contribution Statement

This paper provides the first panel estimates of how teacher pay competitiveness — measured as the ratio of local private-sector earnings to nationally set STPCD pay — affects student value-added in England. We exploit within-LA temporal variation created by differential private-sector wage growth against a nationally constrained teacher pay schedule, complemented by a Bartik IV and academy triple-difference. The key novel contribution is the deprivation gradient: competitiveness effects are concentrated entirely in high-FSM schools.
