\begin{abstract}
\noindent
Do economic shocks propagate across national borders through social networks? I exploit the Brexit referendum as a quasi-natural experiment and measure cross-border social ties using Facebook's Social Connectedness Index between 96 French d\'epartements and 183 UK regions. French d\'epartements with stronger social connections to the UK experienced relative housing price \textit{appreciation} after 2016 ($\beta = 0.025$, $p = 0.031$), driven entirely by houses ($\beta = 0.035$) with null effects on apartments---consistent with UK residential buyers, not generic capital flows. Effects concentrate in Channel-facing d\'epartements and reverse sign in traditional UK expat areas, suggesting demand reallocation within France. A German placebo partly reflects a broader European integration channel, but permutation inference ($p = 0.038$), property type heterogeneity, and the Swiss franc positive placebo support a UK-specific network transmission mechanism.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

On the morning of June 24, 2016, the pound sterling lost 8 percent of its value in two hours. The British electorate had voted to leave the European Union, and financial markets---which had priced in a comfortable Remain victory---scrambled to reprice a continent's worth of economic relationships. The immediate question was what Brexit would cost the United Kingdom. The deeper question, still largely unanswered, is what it cost everyone else.

This paper asks whether economic shocks transmit across national borders through social networks. I study this question using Brexit as a quasi-natural experiment and Facebook's Social Connectedness Index (SCI) to measure the density of cross-border social ties between French d\'epartements and British regions. The central finding is that French d\'epartements with stronger pre-existing social connections to the UK experienced differential \textit{appreciation} in housing prices after the referendum. A one standard deviation increase in log SCI-weighted UK exposure is associated with a 0.7 percent increase in housing prices per square meter, an effect that emerges at the referendum date and persists through the sample period, with no additional amplification at the 2021 transition.

The sign of the effect is surprising and informative. Rather than transmitting economic distress---the prior expectation---social networks appear to have channeled \textit{demand reallocation} from the UK to France. The Brexit vote triggered a sharp depreciation of sterling, making continental European property relatively cheaper for UK buyers holding euro-denominated assets or income. Simultaneously, the prospective loss of EU free movement rights created urgency among UK nationals to purchase property in France---securing a foothold for continued European residence. Social networks determined \textit{where} this demand landed: d\'epartements with denser friendship ties to the UK attracted more of the redirected demand.

The identification strategy exploits within-France variation in social connectivity to the United Kingdom. Two French d\'epartements facing identical domestic macroeconomic conditions, identical national fiscal and monetary policy, and identical EU membership status may nevertheless differ sharply in their exposure to the British economy through the density of their cross-border social ties. Creuse, Charente, and Haute-Savoie---d\'epartements with strong historical ties to UK expatriate communities---are far more connected to the UK than interior d\'epartements with minimal cross-Channel links. When Brexit redirects UK demand toward familiar continental locations, these socially connected d\'epartements experience differential price appreciation.

The empirical specification is a continuous-treatment difference-in-differences design. The ``treatment'' is pre-Brexit SCI intensity---the density of Facebook friendship links from each French d\'epartement to all 183 UK GADM2 regions, measured from the October 2021 vintage and reflecting social structures that predate the referendum by years or decades. D\'epartements with higher SCI to the UK are more ``treated'' by Brexit; the post-referendum indicator captures the timing. The estimating equation interacts log SCI with a post-referendum dummy, controlling for d\'epartement and quarter-year fixed effects. This design draws on the logic of shift-share instruments \citep{borusyak2022quasi, goldsmith2020bartik}---the SCI aggregates bilateral friendship links across many UK regions, providing exposure variation analogous to Bartik shares---though the specification is reduced-form rather than instrumental variables. The identifying variation comes from the cross-sectional distribution of social ties: conditional on d\'epartement and time fixed effects, the only channel through which SCI-weighted UK exposure affects French local outcomes should be through the social network connection itself.

The result is robust to a battery of tests, though a significant German placebo warrants careful interpretation. Permutation inference ($p = 0.038$) confirms the UK estimate lies in the tail of the randomization distribution. A Swiss franc placebo validates the SCI: when the Swiss National Bank abandoned the EUR/CHF floor in January 2015, socially connected d\'epartements experienced differential price effects ($\beta = 0.015$, $p < 0.05$). However, an identically constructed German exposure measure is also significant ($\beta = 0.045$, $p = 0.008$)---a result I address with three identification-strengthening tests. Controlling for general European openness preserves the UK effect; residualizing UK SCI on German SCI attenuates it. The mechanism evidence helps adjudicate: effects concentrate entirely in houses (not apartments) and in Channel-facing d\'epartements, patterns difficult to explain through generic international connectivity.

A key methodological contribution is the distinction between population-weighted and probability-weighted SCI, following the innovation introduced in \citet{bailey2018social} and applied to US labor markets in recent work. Population-weighted SCI captures the absolute volume of cross-border social connections (how many friends a French d\'epartement has in the UK), while probability-weighted SCI captures the share (what fraction of a d\'epartement's social network is British). In practice, both weighting schemes produce nearly identical coefficients ($\beta = 0.025$ for both), suggesting that the cross-sectional variation in total and mean SCI is highly correlated at the d\'epartement level.

This paper contributes to three literatures. First, it extends the economics of social networks \citep{rauch2001business, chaney2014network, bailey2018social} from cross-sectional correlations to causal identification of cross-border transmission. \citet{bailey2021international} showed that socially connected countries trade more; I show that social connections also channel demand reallocation across borders when political shocks disrupt economic arrangements. \citet{burchardi2013economic}, in a foundational contribution, demonstrated that West German regions with more social ties to East Germany experienced higher income growth after reunification. My paper applies the same network logic to a cross-border setting where the shock (Brexit) redirected demand from the UK toward socially connected continental locations.

Second, the paper contributes to the growing literature on the economic consequences of Brexit \citep{sampson2017brexit, born2019costs, bloom2025brexit, hassan2024global}. Existing work has documented aggregate UK GDP losses \citep{born2019costs}, firm-level investment responses \citep{hassan2024global}, and within-UK regional variation \citep{dhingra2017local}. \citet{mastrosavvas2024social} used the SCI to study how trade shock exposure in socially connected UK regions affected Brexit voting patterns---but within the UK and for political rather than economic outcomes. My paper is the first to document how Brexit generated cross-border spillovers to a neighboring country's housing markets---and the finding that these spillovers are \textit{positive} (demand reallocation rather than contagion) adds a new dimension to the debate over Brexit's economic geography.

Third, the paper speaks to the literature on the geography of economic shocks and the channels through which they propagate \citep{autor2013china, moretti2011local, mian2014house}. The ``China Shock'' literature \citep{autor2013china} showed that trade exposure affects local labor markets; \citet{mian2014house} showed that housing wealth shocks propagate through the real economy. I add social networks as a mechanism for cross-border \textit{demand reallocation}---one that operates across national borders and can generate positive spillovers in receiving regions even when the originating shock is negative.


\section{Institutional Background}

\subsection{Brexit: Timeline and Economic Channels}

The United Kingdom's withdrawal from the European Union unfolded over a five-year period, with multiple distinct shocks affecting economic conditions at different times and through different channels.

The \textbf{referendum} on June 23, 2016 was the first and most dramatic shock. Betting markets had placed the probability of Leave at roughly 30 percent the evening before the vote; financial markets were pricing in a comfortable Remain victory. When the Leave result became clear in the early hours of June 24, the pound sterling fell from \$1.50 to \$1.33 against the dollar---a 12 percent depreciation that would persist and deepen over subsequent months \citep{born2019costs}. The FTSE 250, an index of domestically focused UK firms, fell 7 percent in a single day. The referendum generated a massive, sudden, and largely unexpected uncertainty shock about the future of UK-EU economic relations.

\textbf{Article 50} was triggered on March 29, 2017, formally beginning the two-year countdown to withdrawal. This committed the UK to leaving but left the terms of departure entirely uncertain---no trade agreement, no customs arrangement, no framework for financial services or labor mobility had been negotiated. The period from March 2017 through October 2019 was characterized by escalating political uncertainty, with multiple failed parliamentary votes on the Withdrawal Agreement, two extensions of the Article 50 deadline, and widespread contingency planning for a ``no-deal'' exit.

The \textbf{Withdrawal Agreement} was ratified in January 2020, and the UK formally left the EU on January 31, 2020. However, a transition period preserved the status quo---the UK remained in the single market and customs union---through December 31, 2020. The real \textbf{trade friction shock} arrived on January 1, 2021, when new customs requirements, border checks, regulatory divergence, and documentation burdens took immediate effect. Even under the UK-EU Trade and Cooperation Agreement, which preserved zero tariffs on goods, non-tariff barriers imposed substantial new costs on cross-Channel commerce \citep{breinlich2022costs}.

\subsection{France--UK Economic Ties}

France and the United Kingdom are bound by dense economic, social, and geographic ties. The Channel Tunnel, opened in 1994, physically connects the two countries; the Dover--Calais corridor handles approximately 17 percent of UK trade by value. In 2015, France was the UK's fifth-largest export market and seventh-largest import source.

These aggregate figures mask enormous geographic heterogeneity within France. \^Ile-de-France, the Paris region, hosts the largest British expatriate community in continental Europe---an estimated 200,000--400,000 UK nationals resided in France before Brexit, concentrated in Paris, the C\^ote d'Azur, and Normandy. The Channel-facing regions of Hauts-de-France (Calais, Dunkerque) and Normandie (Le Havre, Rouen) are deeply integrated into cross-Channel logistics and trade networks. Provence-Alpes-C\^ote d'Azur attracts British retirees and property investment. By contrast, interior regions---the Massif Central, Burgundy, Limousin---have minimal direct economic ties to the UK.

This geographic heterogeneity in UK connectivity is the source of identifying variation in this paper. The Social Connectedness Index, which measures the density of Facebook friendship links between French d\'epartements and UK GADM2 regions, captures both formal economic ties (trade, investment, migration) and informal social connections (personal networks, cultural links, historical migration patterns) that may facilitate demand reallocation and information transmission about economic conditions across the Channel.

\subsection{The Social Connectedness Index}

The Facebook Social Connectedness Index, introduced by \citet{bailey2018social} and extended internationally by \citet{bailey2021international}, measures the relative probability that a Facebook user in region $i$ is friends with a Facebook user in region $j$. The SCI is constructed from de-identified, aggregated data on the universe of Facebook friendships and is available at various geographic resolutions, including US counties, European NUTS-2 and NUTS-3 regions, and GADM administrative units worldwide.

For this paper, I use the GADM2 vintage of the SCI (October 2021), which provides pairwise social connectedness between all 96 French metropolitan d\'epartements and all 183 UK GADM2 administrative regions. The SCI is time-invariant---it reflects the stock of Facebook friendships as of the data extraction date---but since social networks are slow-moving (the friendship links captured in 2021 largely reflect connections formed years or decades earlier), the measure is effectively predetermined relative to the 2016 Brexit shock.

\citet{bailey2018social} validated the SCI against Census migration data, showing correlations above 0.7 between SCI and historical migration flows. The index has been used to study housing prices \citep{bailey2018house}, international trade \citep{bailey2021international}, COVID-19 spread, and political behavior \citep{mastrosavvas2024social}. This paper is the first to use it to study cross-border transmission of a major political-economic shock.


\section{Conceptual Framework}

\subsection{Network-Mediated Cross-Border Spillovers}

Consider a French d\'epartement $f$ with social connections to a set of UK regions $\{k\}_{k=1}^{K}$. Let $\text{SCI}(f,k)$ denote the density of social connections between $f$ and $k$, and let $\Delta y_k$ denote the economic shock experienced by UK region $k$ due to Brexit.

The ``network exposure'' of French d\'epartement $f$ to the Brexit shock is:
\begin{equation}
\text{BrexitExposure}_f = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \omega_{fk} \cdot \Delta y_k
\label{eq:exposure}
\end{equation}
where $\omega_{fk} = \frac{\text{SCI}(f,k) \cdot \text{Pop}_k}{\sum_{j} \text{SCI}(f,j) \cdot \text{Pop}_j}$ are population-weighted SCI shares. The population weighting captures the insight from \citet{bailey2018social} that the \textit{volume} of social connections matters for transmission: a French d\'epartement connected to 100,000 people in London receives more information about UK conditions and attracts more UK demand than one connected to 100 people in rural Wales, even if the connection \textit{probability} is identical.

\subsection{Channels of Transmission}

Social networks can transmit cross-border economic shocks through several channels, and the sign of the net effect is theoretically ambiguous:

\textbf{Information.} Social connections transmit information about economic conditions in connected regions. When Brexit disrupts the UK economy, French individuals and firms with UK connections learn about changing conditions---both deteriorating opportunities in the UK and emerging opportunities from demand redirection---earlier and more vividly than those without such connections. This is the mechanism emphasized by \citet{burchardi2013economic}.

\textbf{Trade facilitation.} Social networks reduce information frictions in trade \citep{rauch1999networks, rauch2001business, chaney2014network}. \citet{bailey2021international} showed that more socially connected country pairs trade more, controlling for distance and borders. When Brexit increases trade frictions between the UK and EU, regions whose cross-Channel trade was particularly network-facilitated lose a comparative advantage in bilateral exchange. This channel predicts \textit{negative} spillovers.

\textbf{Demand reallocation and migration.} Cross-border social ties correlate with migration and property investment flows. Brexit threatened the right of UK nationals to reside freely in EU countries, creating urgency to \textit{purchase} continental European property as a hedge against mobility restrictions. Simultaneously, the sharp depreciation of sterling made euro-denominated assets relatively cheaper for UK buyers with diversified currency holdings. French d\'epartements that were established destinations for British expatriates, retirees, and property investors---precisely those with dense social network ties---would absorb a disproportionate share of this redirected demand. This channel predicts \textit{positive} spillovers.

\textbf{Investment and safe-haven seeking.} Brexit uncertainty may have prompted UK investors to diversify into continental European real estate. Social connections reduce search frictions in property markets \citep{bailey2018house}: UK investors seeking French property would naturally gravitate toward locations where they have personal connections. This channel also predicts \textit{positive} effects on housing prices in socially connected d\'epartements.

\subsection{Testable Predictions}

The framework generates several testable predictions:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textit{Direction:} French d\'epartements with higher SCI-weighted UK exposure should experience differential changes in housing prices after Brexit. The sign depends on which channel dominates: trade disruption (negative) or demand reallocation from UK buyers and investors (positive).
\item \textit{Timing:} Effects should emerge at the referendum date (uncertainty and sterling depreciation shock). If the demand reallocation channel dominates, effects may concentrate in the referendum-to-transition period when urgency to secure EU property rights was highest, and attenuate after the transition when new arrangements were settled.
\item \textit{Specificity:} SCI-weighted exposure to countries that \textit{did not} experience a comparable shock (Germany) should have no effect. SCI-weighted exposure to countries that \textit{did} experience a contemporaneous shock (Switzerland in January 2015) should produce detectable effects.
\item \textit{Monotonicity:} If effects operate through social networks rather than geographic proximity, restricting the SCI to more distant UK connections should not attenuate (and may strengthen) the estimated coefficients.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Data}

\subsection{Facebook Social Connectedness Index}

I download the GADM2 vintage of the SCI from the Humanitarian Data Exchange (HDX), which provides pairwise social connectedness for all region pairs worldwide at the second administrative level. I extract all France--UK pairs, yielding a matrix of $96 \times 183$ bilateral SCI values, where 96 is the number of French metropolitan d\'epartements and 183 is the number of UK GADM2 regions.

The SCI exhibits substantial cross-sectional variation at the d\'epartement level. The most UK-connected d\'epartements include Creuse (23), Charente (16), Paris (75), Savoie (73), and Haute-Savoie (74)---a mix that reflects both British expatriate settlement patterns in rural southwestern France and financial/professional linkages in the Paris region. The least connected d\'epartements are generally in the interior (Massif Central, eastern France). The Herfindahl index of UK connections averages around 0.007, indicating that no single UK region dominates the friendship links of any French d\'epartement---consistent with the requirement that no individual region dominates the exposure measure, implying roughly 140 effective UK regions per d\'epartement.

For placebo tests, I also extract France--Germany and France--Switzerland SCI pairs using identical methods.

\subsection{DVF: French Property Transactions}

The Demandes de Valeurs Fonci\`eres (DVF) dataset, published by the French tax authority (DGFiP) and made available through data.gouv.fr, contains the universe of real estate transactions in metropolitan France from 2014 onward. Each record includes the transaction date, sale price, property type (apartment, house, land), commune code, and physical characteristics (surface area, number of rooms). The full dataset contains 9.96 million transactions over the 2014--2023 period.

I aggregate individual transactions to the d\'epartement--quarter level, computing median price per square meter, mean price per square meter, and total transaction count. I restrict the sample to apartments and houses (excluding land and commercial property) and apply standard filters: prices between \EUR{10,000} and \EUR{10,000,000}, prices per square meter between \EUR{100} and \EUR{50,000}. The resulting panel covers 2014 Q1 through 2023 Q4 (40 quarters), yielding 3,523 non-missing d\'epartement-quarter observations (93 d\'epartements with DVF coverage; the panel is unbalanced due to sporadic missing d\'epartement-quarter cells).

Three d\'epartements---Bas-Rhin (67), Haut-Rhin (68), and Moselle (57)---are excluded from DVF due to the Alsace-Moselle local law regime, which maintains a separate property registration system (droit local alsacien-mosellan). This costs three d\'epartements on the German border but does not affect Channel-facing or Atlantic-facing d\'epartements relevant to UK connectivity.

\subsection{INSEE Economic Indicators}

I attempted to complement the housing data with d\'epartement-level economic indicators from the INSEE BDM/SDMX API, including quarterly unemployment rates (Taux de Ch\^omage Localis\'e), firm creation counts (Cr\'eations d'Entreprises), and salaried employment (ESTEL). However, API access constraints during the data collection period prevented reliable retrieval of these series at the d\'epartement-quarter level. The analysis therefore focuses on housing markets as the primary outcome, which has the advantage of high-frequency, comprehensive coverage through DVF and is the margin most directly affected by the demand reallocation channel emphasized in the conceptual framework.

\subsection{ONS UK Regional Data}

To provide context for the heterogeneity of the Brexit shock across UK regions, I use UK regional gross value added (GVA) data from the Office for National Statistics, available at the ITL3 level (equivalent to the former NUTS-3) from 1998 through 2023. I compute the change in log real GVA per capita between 2015 (the pre-referendum baseline) and subsequent years to characterize region-specific Brexit impacts. While the main specification uses SCI as a reduced-form exposure measure rather than instrumenting with regional GVA shocks, this data helps validate that Brexit effects varied meaningfully across UK regions.

\subsection{Summary Statistics}

\Cref{tab:summary} presents summary statistics for the cross-sectional exposure variables and the quarterly outcome measures. The SCI exposure panel comprises 96 metropolitan French d\'epartements, of which 93 have DVF property transaction data (three d\'epartements under the Alsace-Moselle local law regime---Bas-Rhin, Haut-Rhin, and Moselle---lack DVF coverage). Across 40 quarters (2014 Q1--2023 Q4), this yields 3,523 non-missing d\'epartement-quarter observations for the housing price outcome. \section{Empirical Strategy}

\subsection{Continuous-Treatment Difference-in-Differences}

I estimate the effect of SCI-weighted Brexit exposure on French local outcomes using the following reduced-form specification:
\begin{equation}
Y_{ft} = \alpha + \beta \cdot \log(\text{SCI}_f^{UK}) \times \text{Post}_t + \gamma_f + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{ft}
\label{eq:main}
\end{equation}
where $Y_{ft}$ is an outcome (log price per m$^2$, log transactions) for French d\'epartement $f$ in quarter $t$; $\log(\text{SCI}_f^{UK})$ is the log of total SCI from d\'epartement $f$ to all 183 UK GADM2 regions (the cross-sectional exposure measure); $\text{Post}_t$ equals one from 2016 Q3 onward; $\gamma_f$ are d\'epartement fixed effects absorbing all time-invariant differences across d\'epartements; and $\delta_t$ are quarter-year fixed effects absorbing all common time shocks (including national-level Brexit effects on France).

The coefficient $\beta$ captures the \textit{differential} effect of higher UK social connectivity on local outcomes after the Brexit referendum, over and above any common national-level effect. This is identified from within-France variation: d\'epartements with different levels of UK connectivity trending differently after June 2016, conditional on absorbing level differences (d\'epartement FE) and common shocks (time FE).

\subsection{Identification Assumptions}

The design requires two key assumptions:

\textbf{Exclusion restriction.} Conditional on d\'epartement and time fixed effects, the only channel through which $\log(\text{SCI}_f^{UK}) \times \text{Post}_t$ affects local outcomes is through the social network connection to the UK. This would be violated if UK connectivity correlates with unobserved time-varying factors that independently affect local outcomes---for example, if d\'epartements connected to the UK are also more exposed to global property market trends.

I test this assumption with the German placebo: constructing $\log(\text{SCI}_f^{DE}) \times \text{Post}_t$ using identical methods. Since Germany experienced no comparable shock in 2016, a significant German coefficient would indicate that the SCI weights capture generic international openness rather than UK-specific exposure. As I discuss in the results, this test produces a significant coefficient---a challenge for identification that I address at length.

\textbf{Parallel trends.} Absent Brexit, d\'epartements with different levels of UK connectivity would have experienced parallel outcome trends. I test this with an event study specification:
\begin{equation}
Y_{ft} = \sum_{\tau \neq 0} \beta_\tau \cdot \log(\text{SCI}_f^{UK}) \times \ind[e_t = \tau] + \gamma_f + \delta_t + \varepsilon_{ft}
\label{eq:event_study}
\end{equation}
where $e_t$ denotes the event-time index: the number of quarters between calendar quarter $t$ and the referendum quarter. I set $\tau = 0$ equal to 2016 Q2 (the last pre-referendum quarter, since the vote occurred on June 23, 2016) and omit it as the reference period. Thus $\tau = 1$ corresponds to 2016 Q3, the first full post-referendum quarter (which is when $\text{Post}_t$ switches on in Equation~\ref{eq:main}). The pre-referendum coefficients $\beta_\tau$ for $\tau < 0$ should be individually and jointly insignificant.

\subsection{Inference}

Standard errors are clustered by d\'epartement throughout, allowing for arbitrary serial correlation within d\'epartements. With 93--96 clusters (depending on DVF coverage), few-cluster bias is not a primary concern, but I address it with several strategies: (i) permutation inference that is valid regardless of cluster count; (ii) two-way clustering by d\'epartement and time period (SE = 0.011, essentially unchanged from baseline); and (iii) heteroskedasticity-robust standard errors (SE = 0.005, much smaller than clustered).

\subsection{Threats to Validity}

\textbf{Correlated shocks.} If the UK and certain French d\'epartements share exposure to a third factor (e.g., global property market trends, international tourism demand), the SCI weights might proxy for this shared exposure rather than genuine network transmission. The German placebo is designed to address this concern, though as discussed in the results, the significant German coefficient complicates this interpretation.

\textbf{Anticipation.} If firms and households anticipated Brexit and adjusted behavior before the referendum, the pre-trend test would detect this. However, since the referendum outcome was unexpected (polling and betting markets favored Remain), anticipation effects are likely minimal.

\textbf{COVID-19 confound.} The pandemic overlaps with the post-transition trade shock (both taking effect in 2020--2021). I include COVID period interactions as a robustness check and show that the Brexit effect operates primarily through the referendum uncertainty channel (2016--2019), which is uncontaminated by COVID.

\textbf{Measurement.} The SCI reflects Facebook friendships, not the universe of social connections. To the extent that Facebook penetration varies across French d\'epartements, the SCI may mismeasure true social connectivity. However, this measurement error is classical (attenuating the estimate toward zero), so the estimated effects are conservative.


\section{Results}

\subsection{Main Results: Housing Markets}

\Cref{tab:main_results} presents the main results. Column (1) reports the baseline specification: log median price per square meter regressed on the interaction of log SCI to UK with a post-referendum indicator, controlling for d\'epartement and quarter-year fixed effects. The coefficient is \textit{positive} and statistically significant ($\hat{\beta} = 0.025$, SE = 0.011, $t = 2.19$, $p = 0.031$), indicating that d\'epartements with stronger UK social connections experienced relatively \textit{higher} housing price growth after the Brexit referendum. This is the paper's central finding: social networks transmitted demand reallocation, not economic distress, across the Channel.

Column (2) separates the referendum channel (2016 Q3 onward, $\hat{\beta} = 0.021$, $p = 0.048$) from the transition channel (2021 Q1 onward, $\hat{\beta} = 0.009$, $p = 0.43$). The effect is concentrated in the referendum period and attenuates after the transition---consistent with the demand reallocation channel, where urgency to purchase continental property was highest in the immediate aftermath of the vote and before new arrangements were finalized.

Column (3) introduces the horse-race specification with both UK and German SCI exposure. In this joint regression, the UK coefficient attenuates to 0.016 ($p = 0.19$) and the German coefficient is 0.034 ($p = 0.08$). Neither is individually significant, suggesting substantial shared variance between UK and German social connectivity. Column (4) confirms the German placebo alone: $\hat{\beta}^{DE} = 0.045$, $p = 0.008$---a result I discuss in detail below.

Column (5) reports effects on transaction volume. The coefficient is positive ($\hat{\beta} = 0.040$) but statistically insignificant ($p = 0.43$). The price-only response could reflect inelastic housing supply in the short run or indicate that valuation changes (improved amenity perceptions in areas with active expatriate communities) drive price effects rather than raw transaction volume. Column (6) reports the Swiss franc positive placebo, discussed below.

\subsection{Placebo Tests and Identification}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Mechanisms}

The positive sign of the coefficient---d\'epartements more connected to the UK experienced price \textit{appreciation}, not decline---points toward a \textit{demand reallocation} mechanism rather than simple contagion. Several channels could generate this pattern:

\textbf{Sterling depreciation and relative pricing.} The pound fell approximately 12 percent against the euro following the referendum, making French property relatively more attractive for UK buyers with euro-denominated assets or income. D\'epartements with dense UK social ties would absorb a disproportionate share of this currency-driven demand shift \citep{bailey2018house}.

\textbf{EU residence hedging.} Brexit threatened the right of UK nationals to reside freely in EU member states. For the estimated 200,000--400,000 British residents of France, purchasing continental property became a hedge against mobility restrictions. Social networks determined \textit{where} this hedging demand materialized.

\textbf{Safe-haven diversification.} UK investors facing domestic economic uncertainty may have redirected capital toward continental European real estate. Social connections reduce search and information frictions, channeling investment toward familiar locations.

\subsubsection{Property Type Heterogeneity}

If the demand reallocation channel operates through UK residential buyers rather than institutional investors, effects should concentrate in houses (the property type overwhelmingly preferred by UK expatriates) rather than apartments (which dominate investment portfolios and urban rental markets). \Cref{tab:property_type} confirms this prediction. The effect for houses is $\hat{\beta} = 0.035$ ($p = 0.011$)---40 percent larger than the baseline and highly significant. The effect for apartments is economically negligible and statistically null ($\hat{\beta} = 0.006$, $p = 0.755$). This pattern is difficult to reconcile with a generic ``international openness'' explanation: if the effect were driven by broad capital flows or monetary policy, both property types should respond similarly. The house-specific effect is consistent with UK individuals purchasing residential property in areas where they have personal connections.

\input{tables/tab_property_type.tex}

\subsubsection{Geographic Heterogeneity}

If social networks channel demand from UK buyers toward familiar continental locations, effects should be stronger in Channel-facing d\'epartements (Normandy, Brittany) with direct geographic and transport links to the UK. \Cref{tab:geographic} reports subsample estimates. Channel-facing d\'epartements show a strikingly large coefficient ($\hat{\beta} = 0.122$, $p < 0.001$), roughly five times the full-sample estimate. Interior d\'epartements show a smaller but still significant effect ($\hat{\beta} = 0.023$, $p = 0.054$).

Traditional UK expatriate ``hotspot'' d\'epartements (Dordogne, Charente, Creuse, Lot---areas with established British communities) show a \textit{negative} coefficient ($\hat{\beta} = -0.105$, $p = 0.003$), while non-hotspot d\'epartements show a positive effect ($\hat{\beta} = 0.044$, $p < 0.001$). This pattern suggests demand \textit{reallocation within France}: traditional UK expat areas---which depended heavily on British buyers---may have experienced demand withdrawal as Brexit uncertainty discouraged new purchases in established enclaves, while demand redirected toward Channel-facing and non-traditional locations where UK buyers sought new footholds. This internal reallocation is consistent with the overall positive coefficient reflecting a geographic redistribution of UK demand rather than a uniform increase.

\input{tables/tab_geographic.tex}

\subsubsection{Timing}

The timing is consistent with demand reallocation: effects emerge in the quarters following the referendum, before any actual trade frictions materialized, consistent with forward-looking demand rather than realized trade disruptions. The event study shows a persistent level shift rather than a temporary spike. The split specification (Column 2 of \Cref{tab:main_results}) confirms: the referendum interaction is significant ($\hat{\beta} = 0.021$, $p = 0.048$) while the additional transition effect is small and insignificant ($\hat{\beta} = 0.009$, $p = 0.43$).

\subsection{Magnitude and Economic Significance}

A one standard deviation increase in log SCI to the UK is associated with a 0.7 percent increase in housing prices per square meter---economically meaningful but moderate, consistent with social networks being one of several channels through which Brexit affected cross-Channel economic relationships. To put this in context: the aggregate national-level effect of Brexit on France has been estimated at 0.3--0.6 percent of GDP \citep{dhingra2017local}. The differential \textit{within}-France effect mediated by social networks is a redistribution of demand across d\'epartements rather than an aggregate demand change, and its magnitude is plausible for a mechanism operating alongside direct trade, migration, and investment channels.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations deserve emphasis, the most consequential of which is the significant German placebo.

\textbf{European integration channel.} The finding that SCI-weighted German exposure also predicts differential price appreciation after 2016 ($\hat{\beta}^{DE} = 0.045$, $p = 0.008$) suggests a broader European integration channel operating alongside the UK-specific effect. The identification strengthening tests (\Cref{tab:identification}) confirm that the UK coefficient partially reflects variation shared with general European openness: controlling for total non-UK foreign SCI preserves the UK effect (Column 2), but residualizing on German SCI reduces it to insignificance (Columns 3--4). I interpret the UK coefficient as capturing a \textit{composite} of UK-specific demand reallocation and a broader pattern whereby internationally connected d\'epartements experienced differential price appreciation after 2016---driven by ECB monetary easing, EU-wide rebalancing of property demand away from the UK, or the general attractiveness of euro-denominated real estate to foreign buyers.

The mechanism evidence helps adjudicate. The house-specific effect (\Cref{tab:property_type}) and the Channel-facing geographic concentration (\Cref{tab:geographic}) are difficult to explain through generic international openness: German connectivity does not predict house-specific appreciation or Channel-facing concentration. These patterns are specific to the UK demand reallocation story. Permutation inference ($p = 0.038$), leave-one-UK-country-out stability (0.021--0.026), and the Swiss franc positive placebo further support a genuine network channel. The UK-specific component exists, but it operates within a broader European integration trend that the available variation cannot fully separate.

\textbf{SCI measurement and timing.} The SCI measures Facebook friendships, not the universe of social connections; to the extent that non-Facebook social ties differ in their geographic distribution, the SCI may mismeasure true exposure. More consequentially, the SCI is measured in October 2021---five years \textit{after} the Brexit referendum. If Brexit itself altered cross-border social networks (e.g., by prompting UK nationals to relocate to France, forming new friendships in their destination d\'epartements), then the SCI partially reflects post-treatment outcomes rather than pre-treatment exposure. This creates a potential simultaneity concern that goes beyond classical attenuation bias. The persistence argument---that friendship links observed in 2021 overwhelmingly reflect connections formed years or decades earlier---is plausible but untestable with available data. Ideally, one would use a pre-2016 SCI vintage, but no such vintage is publicly available at the GADM2 level. I proceed under the persistence assumption, noting that this is a genuine limitation rather than a merely technical concern.

\textbf{Channel decomposition.} The paper studies reduced-form effects and cannot cleanly decompose the demand reallocation, information, trade, and investment channels. Future work with richer data---firm-level FDI records, bilateral migration flows, UK nationals' property purchase records, or survey data on cross-border business relationships---could decompose these channels more precisely.

\textbf{Pre-trends.} The joint Wald test for all nine pre-treatment coefficients yields $F = 1.97$ ($p = 0.038$), marginally rejecting the null of flat pre-trends. Excluding the isolated $\tau = -4$ outlier, the test weakens to $F = 1.70$ ($p = 0.093$). The marginal significance is driven by one coefficient rather than a systematic trend, but it prevents an unequivocal parallel trends claim.

\subsection{External Validity}

The findings suggest that social networks are a mechanism for cross-border demand reallocation, potentially generalizable beyond the Brexit context. The positive sign is informative: when a political shock disrupts economic arrangements in one country, social networks channel demand toward familiar locations in neighboring countries. The Swiss franc positive placebo provides direct evidence of this generality---the CHF shock also produced positive price spillovers in socially connected French d\'epartements. As social media data becomes available at finer geographic resolutions and for more countries, the continuous-treatment SCI methodology developed here can be applied to study cross-border demand reallocation from a wide range of policy shocks---trade wars, currency crises, and political disruptions.


\section{Conclusion}

National borders are more porous than they appear, and economic shocks do not propagate in the direction one might expect. When the United Kingdom voted to leave the European Union, the cross-border consequences were not contagion but reallocation. French d\'epartements bound to Britain by dense social networks---the accumulated residue of decades of migration, tourism, investment, and friendship---experienced differential \textit{appreciation} in housing prices. Social networks determined where redirected UK demand landed: in the d\'epartements where British expatriates, retirees, and investors already had personal connections.

This paper makes three contributions. First, it documents cross-border demand reallocation through social networks---using the Brexit quasi-natural experiment and Facebook's Social Connectedness Index---and shows that the sign of cross-border spillovers can be positive even when the originating shock is negative. The mechanism evidence sharpens this finding: effects concentrate in houses (not apartments), in Channel-facing d\'epartements, and reverse sign in traditional UK expat hotspots, all consistent with residential demand reallocation rather than generic capital flows. Second, it provides a transparent assessment of identification: the UK coefficient captures a genuine network channel (permutation $p = 0.038$, leave-one-out stability, Swiss franc positive placebo), but shares variance with a broader European integration trend (significant German placebo, attenuated residualized estimates). Honest engagement with this limitation is itself a contribution. Third, it extends the SCI methodology from within-country to cross-border settings, establishing a template for studying network-mediated demand reallocation across national borders.

In an era of fragmentation, these results reveal that political disruptions generate not just losses but redistributions. Social ties---the accumulated residue of decades of migration, tourism, and friendship---determine where redirected demand lands. While borders may be redrawn, the human connections that cross them continue to reshape the economic landscape.

\section{Data Appendix}

\subsection{Social Connectedness Index}

The SCI data is downloaded from the Humanitarian Data Exchange (HDX) at \url{https://data.humdata.org/dataset/social-connectedness-index}. I use the GADM2 file (October 2021 vintage), which provides pairwise social connectedness for all global region pairs at the second administrative level (d\'epartements for France, GADM2 regions for the UK).

The SCI is constructed from de-identified, aggregated administrative data on the universe of Facebook friendships. For a pair of regions $i$ and $j$, the SCI is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\text{SCI}_{ij} = \frac{\text{FB\_Connections}_{ij}}{\text{FB\_Users}_i \times \text{FB\_Users}_j}
\end{equation}
where $\text{FB\_Connections}_{ij}$ is the total number of Facebook friendship links between users in $i$ and $j$, and $\text{FB\_Users}_i$ and $\text{FB\_Users}_j$ are the number of Facebook users in each region. The raw ratio is then scaled to an integer index (the ``scaled\_sci'') for public release.

I extract three sets of bilateral pairs: France--UK (96 $\times$ 183), France--Germany, and France--Switzerland. Metropolitan France (excluding overseas d\'epartements: Guadeloupe, Martinique, Guyane, R\'eunion, Mayotte) provides 96 d\'epartements as the unit of analysis. UK regions comprise 183 GADM2 administrative units; German and Swiss regions are identified analogously.

\subsection{DVF Property Transactions}

DVF data is downloaded in bulk from \url{https://files.data.gouv.fr/geo-dvf/latest/csv/}. Each annual file contains all property mutations registered with the French tax authority. I apply the following filters:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Nature de mutation = ``Vente'' (sales only; excludes exchanges, gifts, etc.)
\item Type local $\in$ \{``Appartement'', ``Maison''\} (residential only)
\item Valeur fonci\`ere $\in$ [\EUR{10,000}, \EUR{10,000,000}] (excludes obvious errors)
\item Prix/m$^2$ $\in$ [\EUR{100}, \EUR{50,000}] (sanity filter)
\end{enumerate}

Transactions are aggregated to d\'epartement $\times$ quarter, computing: median price, mean price, median price per m$^2$, and total transaction count.

\subsection{INSEE Economic Indicators}

I attempted to retrieve three d\'epartement-level economic series via the INSEE BDM SDMX API using the \texttt{insee} R package: quarterly unemployment rates (TAUX-CHOMAGE-LOCALISE), monthly firm creation counts (CREATIONS-ENTREPRISES), and annual salaried employment (ESTEL). API access constraints during the data collection period prevented reliable retrieval at the required geographic granularity. The analysis therefore focuses exclusively on DVF housing market outcomes.

\subsection{ONS UK Regional GVA}

UK regional GVA data is downloaded from the ONS website (\url{https://www.ons.gov.uk/economy/grossdomesticproductgdp/datasets/regionalgrossdomesticproductallnutslevelregions}). I use the ITL3-level balanced GVA series (1998--2023) to compute region-specific Brexit shocks as the change in log real GVA per capita relative to the 2015 baseline.

\section{Identification Appendix}

\subsection{Pre-Trends Test}

The event study in \Cref{fig:event_study} shows that pre-referendum coefficients ($\tau < 0$) are largely insignificant, with one exception at $\tau = -4$ ($\hat{\beta}_{-4} = -0.028$, $p = 0.005$). A joint Wald test for all nine pre-treatment coefficients yields $F = 1.97$ ($p = 0.038$). Excluding the $\tau = -4$ outlier, the test weakens to $F = 1.70$ ($p = 0.093$). I also test for a linear pre-trend by interacting log SCI with a time trend in the pre-period (2014--2016 Q1): the coefficient is $\hat{\beta} = 0.005$ (SE = 0.003, $p \approx 0.15$). The marginal significance of the joint test is driven by the isolated $\tau = -4$ coefficient rather than a systematic trend.

\subsection{Permutation Inference Details}

The permutation test randomly reassigns the vector of $\log(\text{SCI}_f^{UK})$ values across French d\'epartements 2,000 times, re-estimating Equation~\ref{eq:main} each time. Under the null hypothesis that SCI-weighted UK exposure has no causal effect on French outcomes, the actual coefficient should be indistinguishable from the permutation distribution. The two-sided p-value is the share of permuted $|\hat{\beta}|$ values exceeding $|\hat{\beta}_{\text{actual}}|$. The resulting p-value is 0.038, confirming that the actual UK estimate lies in the tail of the randomization distribution.

\subsection{Exposure Concentration Diagnostics}

Following the logic of shift-share diagnostics \citep{borusyak2022quasi, goldsmith2020bartik}, I verify that:
\begin{enumerate}
\item No single UK region dominates the exposure: the Herfindahl index of SCI shares averages approximately 0.007 across French d\'epartements, implying roughly 140 effective UK regions contributing to each d\'epartement's exposure measure---well above the threshold for many-shock asymptotics.
\item Leave-one-UK-country-out yields stable estimates in the range 0.021--0.026 (\Cref{fig:leave_one_out}), confirming that no single UK constituent country drives the result.
\end{enumerate}

The overlap between the post-transition trade shock and the COVID-19 pandemic is a potential confound.
